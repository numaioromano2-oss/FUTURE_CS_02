Five email samples were analysed using header inspection, sender domain verification, link analysis, and content assessment. Three were confirmed phishing attempts, one was suspicious, and one was a legitimate message included for comparison.

The most common phishing techniques observed were urgency/fear-based language, spoofed sender domains, lookalike URLs, and impersonation of trusted brands. All findings and prevention guidance are documented in this report.

03 — Email Analyses

Sample-by-Sample Findings

E-01 ⚠️ Urgent: Your Account Will Be Locked — Verify Now PHISHING
From:security-alert@account-verify-secure[.]com
Reply-To:noreply@suspiciousdomain[.]ru
SPF:FAIL
DKIM:NONE
DMARC:FAIL
Link:http://secure-account-verify[.]com/login
Dear User,

We noticed suspicious activity on your account. To avoid permanent account suspension,
please verify your details immediately.

👉 Verify Now: http://secure-account-verify[.]com

Failure to verify within 24 hours will result in permanent account lock.

Regards, Security Team

Phishing Indicators Found

  • 🔴
    Fake sender domainaccount-verify-secure[.]com has no relation to any real organisation
  • 🔴
    SPF / DKIM / DMARC all failEmail authentication completely absent — spoofed sender
  • 🔴
    Fear and urgency tactics"Permanent account lock", "24 hours" — designed to panic the user
  • 🔴
    Generic greeting"Dear User" — no real name, mass-sent phishing template
  • 🔴
    Malicious linkURL does not belong to any legitimate service

Business Impact & Advice

If a user clicks the link and enters credentials, attackers gain full account access. This type of email is used for credential harvesting — collecting usernames and passwords at scale.

Action for employees: Do NOT click the link. Report the email to your IT/security team. Verify account status by navigating directly to the official website, never via email links.

Risk Level: ● HIGH — Confirmed credential-harvesting phishing attack

E-02 Your PayPal Payment of $847.00 Has Been Processed PHISHING
From:service@paypa1-support[.]com
Reply-To:refund@paypa1-support[.]com
SPF:SOFTFAIL
DKIM:NONE
DMARC:FAIL
Link:http://paypa1-support[.]com/cancel-payment
Hello,

A payment of $847.00 has been charged to your PayPal account.
If you did not authorise this transaction, call us immediately at +1-888-555-0192
or click below to cancel:

👉 Cancel Payment: http://paypa1-support[.]com/cancel

PayPal Customer Support

Phishing Indicators Found

  • 🔴
    Typosquatted domain — "paypa1" not "paypal"The digit 1 replaces the letter l to fool users at a glance
  • 🔴
    Fabricated financial transactionFake charge designed to cause panic and trigger action
  • 🔴
    Fake phone numberCalling the number connects to attacker's call centre — vishing attack
  • 🔴
    No personalisationReal PayPal emails always include your registered name

Business Impact & Advice

This is a financial phishing attack. If the victim clicks the "cancel" link, they are taken to a fake PayPal login page where their credentials are captured. The phone number leads to a vishing (voice phishing) scam.

Action: Log into PayPal directly by typing paypal.com — never via the email link. Do not call the number provided.

Risk Level: ● HIGH — Financial phishing with vishing component

Phishing Indicators Found

  • 🔴
    Impersonation of internal IT teamSender is not the company's real domain
  • 🔴

  • 🔴
    External reset linkLegitimate IT resets never use third-party domains

Action: Contact IT directly via official internal channels (not reply-to). Never reset passwords via unsolicited email links.

Risk Level: ● HIGH — Corporate credential theft / spear-phishing

05 — Email Header Analysis

What the Headers Tell Us

Email headers contain the full routing and authentication trail of a message. Analysing headers using Google Admin Toolbox or MXToolbox reveals whether an email is genuinely from who it claims to be.

06 — Employee Awareness & Prevention

What Every Employee Should Know

✅ Do's — Safe Email Habits

  • Check the sender's full email address, not just the display name
  • Hover over links before clicking to see the actual destination URL
  • Type website addresses directly into your browser — never via email links
  • Verify urgent requests through a separate communication channel (phone call)
  • Report suspicious emails to your IT/security team immediately
  • Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all accounts
  • Check that emails from banks/PayPal/services use your real name

❌ Don'ts — What to Avoid

  • Never click links in emails claiming your account is locked or suspended
  • Never enter passwords on a page reached via an email link
  • Don't trust an email just because it has a company logo
  • Never call phone numbers provided in unsolicited emails
  • Don't open attachments from unknown or unexpected senders
  • Never forward phishing emails — report them using your email client's tools
  • Don't act immediately on "urgent" requests — take 60 seconds to verify first
🔍

Inspect Before You Click

Hover your mouse over any link in an email. The real destination URL will appear in the bottom bar of your browser. If it doesn't match the expected website, don't click.

🔐

Use MFA on Everything

Even if attackers steal your password via phishing, multi-factor authentication (MFA/2FA) prevents them from logging in. Enable it on every account that supports it.

📞

When in Doubt, Call

If you receive an email claiming to be from IT, your bank, or a supplier requesting urgent action — hang up on the email and call them directly using a number you already know.

🚩

The 3 Red Flag Questions

1. Was this email expected? 2. Does the sender domain exactly match the real company? 3. Is it creating urgency or fear? If any answer is "no" / "yes" — treat it as phishing.

📧

Report, Don't Delete

Always report phishing emails using your email client's "Report Phishing" button before deleting. This helps protect your entire organisation by blocking the sender.

🌐

Check URLs Carefully

Attackers use typosquatting: paypa1.com, arnazon.com, g00gle.com. Always read the full domain carefully. One changed letter = fake site designed to steal your login.

07 — Conclusion

Summary & Recommendations

Of the five email samples analysed, 3 were confirmed phishing attacks, 1 was suspicious, and 1 was legitimate. The phishing emails all relied on the same core techniques: impersonating trusted brands, spoofing sender addresses, using urgency to bypass critical thinking, and embedding lookalike URLs.

The good news is that phishing is highly detectable once users know what to look for. The most effective defences are: checking email authentication (SPF/DKIM/DMARC), verifying sender domains carefully, and never clicking unsolicited links regardless of how urgent the message appears.

Businesses should complement technical controls (spam filters, email authentication) with regular employee awareness training, simulated phishing exercises, and clear reporting procedures.
